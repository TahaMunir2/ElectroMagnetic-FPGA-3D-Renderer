PART 1 — THE PHYSICS

1. Maxwell's Equations — From First Principles
1.1 The Four Equations
Maxwell's equations are four coupled partial differential equations that together describe all
classical electromagnetic phenomena. Every electrical engineering concept — capacitors,
inductors, antennas, waveguides, optical fibres, wireless communication — follows from these
four equations.

1.2 Why These Four Equations Contain Everything
1.2.1 Gauss's Law for E — ∇·E = ρ/ε₀
The divergence of E at any point equals the charge density ρ divided by the permittivity ε₀.
Divergence measures how much a vector field 'spreads out' from a point. Where ρ = 0 (no free
charges, as in your conducting paper between the electrodes), this becomes ∇·E = 0. Combined
with the fact that E = -∇V in static cases, this directly gives Laplace's equation ∇²V = 0. So Laplace
is just a special case of Maxwell — specifically the static, charge-free case.

1.2.2 Gauss's Law for B — ∇·B = 0
Magnetic field lines never start or end anywhere — they always form closed loops. There are no
magnetic monopoles (isolated north or south poles). This is a constraint on B rather than a source
equation — it tells you B must always be divergence-free.

1.2.3 Faraday's Law — ∇×E = -∂B/∂t
This is where electricity and magnetism first become coupled. If B changes in time at some point
in space, a circulating E field is created around that point. This is the physical principle behind

transformers, generators, and wireless power transfer. The curl operator (∇×) measures the
rotation or circulation of a vector field around a point.
In your project: when you drive the electrodes with an AC signal, B is oscillating in time. Faraday's
law says this creates a small circulating E field. At low frequencies (1kHz) this effect is tiny — but
at higher effective frequencies in your FDTD simulation it becomes the dominant physics.

1.2.4 Ampere-Maxwell Law — ∇×B = μ₀J + μ₀ε₀∂E/∂t
Two things create a circulating B field: real current density J (moving charges) and displacement
current μ₀ε₀∂E/∂t (time-varying E field). The displacement current term was Maxwell's crucial
addition to Ampere's original law. Without it, the equations are inconsistent. With it, they predict
electromagnetic waves.

1.3 How Maxwell's Equations Predict EM Waves
Take the curl of Faraday's law:

∇×(∇×E) = -∂(∇×B)/∂t
Use the vector identity ∇×(∇×E) = ∇(∇·E) - ∇²E, and in free space ∇·E = 0:
-∇²E = -∂(∇×B)/∂t
Substitute the Ampere-Maxwell law for ∇×B (with J = 0 in free space):
-∇²E = -μ₀ε₀ ∂²E/∂t²
Rearranging:

∇²E = μ₀ε₀ ∂²E/∂t²
This is the wave equation. Its solution is a wave propagating at speed:
c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀) = 3×10⁸ m/s
Maxwell recognised this as the speed of light and immediately concluded that light is an
electromagnetic wave. This is the central unifying discovery of 19th-century physics. Your FDTD
solver numerically integrates exactly this equation — and will show you electromagnetic waves
propagating across the screen in real time.

1.4 Why Laplace is Not Enough
Laplace's equation ∇²V = 0 describes only electrostatics — the situation where nothing changes
in time. As soon as you have time-varying fields, you need the full Maxwell equations. The new
physics that appears includes:

PART 2 — THE FDTD METHOD

2. FDTD — Finite Difference Time Domain
2.1 The Core Idea
FDTD is a method for numerically solving Maxwell's equations by discretising both space and
time into a grid, then marching the solution forward one time step at a time. It was introduced by
Kane Yee in 1966 and remains the most widely used method for computational electromagnetics.
The key insight is that Maxwell's equations in the time domain tell you how the fields change from
one moment to the next. If you know E and B everywhere right now, the curl equations tell you
exactly how fast they are changing — so you can compute E and B a moment later. Repeat
indefinitely to march the solution forward in time.

2.2 The 2D TE Mode — Your Simulation
Implementing full 3D FDTD would require storing six field components (Ex, Ey, Ez, Bx, By, Bz) at
every point in a 3D volume — far too much memory and compute for your FPGA. Instead you use
2D FDTD in the Transverse Electric (TE) mode.
TE mode assumes the simulation is a 2D cross-section with no variation in the z-direction (∂/∂z =
0). This gives exactly three non-zero field components:
• Ex(x,y,t) — electric field in x-direction
• Ey(x,y,t) — electric field in y-direction
• Bz(x,y,t) — magnetic field in z-direction (perpendicular to the simulation plane)
The other three components (Ez, Bx, By) are identically zero in TE mode. Maxwell's four
equations reduce to three update equations for these three components — exactly what FDTD
needs.

2.3 Deriving the FDTD Update Equations
2.3.1 Starting from Maxwell's Curl Equations
In 2D TE mode with ∂/∂z = 0, the Faraday and Ampere-Maxwell equations expand to:
∂Bz/∂t = ∂Ex/∂y - ∂Ey/∂x

(from Faraday: ∇×E = -∂B/∂t, zcomponent)

∂Ex/∂t = (1/μ₀ε₀) ∂Bz/∂y

(from Ampere-Maxwell: ∇×B = μ₀ε₀∂E/∂t,
x-component)

∂Ey/∂t = -(1/μ₀ε₀) ∂Bz/∂x

(from Ampere-Maxwell: ∇×B = μ₀ε₀∂E/∂t,
y-component)

These three equations are exact. FDTD approximates the spatial and temporal derivatives using
finite differences.

2.3.2 Finite Difference Approximation
A first-order derivative is approximated by a central difference:
∂f/∂x ≈ (f(x + Δx/2) - f(x - Δx/2)) / Δx
This is second-order accurate — the error is proportional to (Δx)². The key point is that the
derivative at position x is evaluated using values at x ± Δx/2 — half a grid cell away. This naturally
leads to the Yee grid where E and B are stored at offset positions.

2.3.3 The Yee Grid
Kane Yee's genius was to store Ex, Ey, and Bz at different positions within each cell, offset by
half a grid spacing. This is not an approximation — it is the natural grid for the central difference
scheme and gives second-order accuracy with no additional cost.

2.3.4 The Leapfrog Time Scheme
E and B are also offset in time by half a time step. B is updated at integer time steps, E is updated
at half-integer time steps. They leapfrog past each other:
t = 0:

E⁰ known (initial conditions)

t = Δt/2: B^(½) computed from E⁰
t = Δt:

E¹ computed from B^(½)

t = 3Δt/2: B^(3/2) computed from E¹
... and so on
This leapfrog scheme is time-reversible and conserves energy — the simulation does not gain or
lose energy artificially over long run times. This is essential for physically correct wave
propagation.

2.4 The Final FDTD Update Equations

Applying the central differences in space and the leapfrog scheme in time to the three curl
equations gives the FDTD update equations. These are what your Verilog implements:

Update 1 — Bz at half time step
Bz^(n+½)(i,j) = Bz^(n-½)(i,j)
+ (Δt/Δx)[Ex^n(i,j+1) - Ex^n(i,j)]
- (Δt/Δy)[Ey^n(i+1,j) - Ey^n(i,j)]

Update 2 — Ex at full time step
Ex^(n+1)(i,j) = Ex^n(i,j)
+ (Δt/μ₀ε₀Δy)[Bz^(n+½)(i,j) - Bz^(n+½)(i,j-1)]

Update 3 — Ey at full time step
Ey^(n+1)(i,j) = Ey^n(i,j)
- (Δt/μ₀ε₀Δx)[Bz^(n+½)(i,j) - Bz^(n+½)(i-1,j)]

What These Equations Say Physically
Each field component at every grid point is updated using only its immediate neighbours (one cell
away) and the value at the previous time step. No global information is needed — the update is
completely local. This is why FDTD is perfectly suited to parallel hardware: every cell can update
simultaneously, independently of all other cells. This is the embarrassingly parallel structure the
project requires.

2.5 The CFL Stability Condition
FDTD is only stable if the time step Δt is small enough relative to the spatial step Δx. Physically,
this means the simulated wave must not travel more than one grid cell per time step. The CourantFriedrichs-Lewy (CFL) condition in 2D is:
Δt ≤ Δx / (c_eff × √2)
Where c_eff is the effective wave speed in your simulation. If you violate this condition, the
simulation becomes numerically unstable — field values grow exponentially and quickly overflow
to infinity. In Verilog, this manifests as fixed-point overflow and the display fills with garbage.
To guarantee stability, choose:
Δt = 0.99 × Δx / (c_eff × √2)

(1% safety margin)

2.6 The Speed Scaling Problem and Solution
2.6.1 The Problem

In free space, c = 3×10⁸ m/s. To see a full wavelength of a 1kHz wave, you would need a
simulation domain 300km wide. This is completely impractical. Even at 1GHz, the wavelength is
30cm — possible but requires a very fine grid to resolve well.

2.6.2 The Solution — Effective Permittivity
In a material with relative permittivity εᵣ, the wave speed is reduced:
c_eff = c / √εᵣ
By choosing a very large εᵣ in the simulation, you can reduce c_eff arbitrarily. This is
mathematically legitimate — you are simulating wave propagation in a high-permittivity medium.
The wave behaviour (reflection, interference, diffraction) is identical in form to free space. Only
the speed changes.

2.6.4 Mapping to Physical Units for Display
When rendering, the normalised simulation coordinates map to pixels directly. The colour

by the ratio of wavelength to domain size, which you have chosen to give interesting patterns.

2.7 Source Injection
To drive the simulation you inject a time-varying field value at one or more source points. This
represents your physical electrodes driving the EM field.

2.7.1 Hard Source
The simplest approach: force Ex or Ey at one grid point to follow a prescribed waveform,
overriding the FDTD update at that point:
Ex(isrc, jsrc) = A × sin(2π × f₀ × n × Δt)
Where n is the time step counter. This is called a hard source. The electrode drives the field
directly with no reflection.

2.7.2 Soft Source (Total-Field/Scattered-Field)
A more sophisticated approach adds the source waveform to the FDTD update rather than
replacing it:
Ex(isrc, jsrc) += A × sin(2π × f₀ × n × Δt)
This allows reflected waves to pass back through the source point without artificial scattering —
more physically correct but slightly more complex to implement. Use hard source first and switch
to soft source as an extension.

2.7.3 Multiple Sources
Multiple sources at different positions create interference patterns. Each source injects at the
same frequency but potentially different phase. A phase difference of π (180°) between two
sources gives destructive interference midway between them. This is directly analogous to
Young's double-slit experiment for light — one of the fundamental demonstrations of wave
behaviour.
In Verilog, multiple sources are handled by a list of (x, y, amplitude, phase) tuples stored in
registers. The source injection logic iterates through this list each time step and adds contributions
to the field.

2.8 Absorbing Boundary Conditions — Perfectly Matched Layer
Without special treatment at the domain boundaries, waves reflect off the edges and propagate
back into the simulation. This creates standing waves that obscure the physics you want to show.
The Perfectly Matched Layer (PML) absorbs outgoing waves with essentially no reflection.

2.8.1 How PML Works
PML adds a thin border region (typically 8–16 cells wide) around the domain where the fields are
gradually attenuated. The attenuation profile is designed so that waves entering the PML are
absorbed without any reflection at the PML interface — the impedance matches perfectly
regardless of angle or frequency.
The attenuation is implemented by adding a conductivity σ to the update equations in the PML
region:
Ex_update_in_PML = Ex × (1 - σΔt/ε₀) + (Δt/ε₀) × (∂Bz/∂y)

The conductivity σ increases from zero at the inner PML boundary to a maximum at the outer
edge, following a polynomial profile (typically cubic) to minimise reflections at the interface.

2.8.2 Simplified PML for FPGA
Full PML requires splitting the field components (Ex becomes Exy + Exz in 3D) which doubles
memory requirements. For 2D TE mode a simpler approach works well: the Convolutional PML
(CPML) or simply a graded conductivity profile applied to the standard update equations. In
practice for your project, use a simple exponential decay:
field_at_boundary = field × decay_factor^(distance_from_edge)
Where decay_factor ≈ 0.90–0.95 per cell in the boundary region (8 cells wide). This is not perfect
PML but gives acceptable absorption for a teaching demonstration and is trivial to implement —
just a multiplication by a stored coefficient at each boundary cell.

3.1.1 Memory Access Pattern
Each FDTD update at pixel (i,j) requires reading up to 4 neighbouring values: (i,j), (i±1,j), (i,j±1).
This creates a memory bandwidth challenge — you need multiple simultaneous reads. The
solution is to use dual-port BRAMs: one port for reading the current value, one port for writing the
updated value. By processing rows sequentially, neighbouring values can be cached in registers,
minimising redundant BRAM accesses.

3.1.2 Ping-Pong Buffering
While the FDTD engine is computing the next time step (writing to buffer B), the renderer reads
the current time step (reading from buffer A). After the FDTD step completes, the roles swap. This
means the renderer always has a complete, consistent field snapshot to render — never a halfupdated frame.

3.2 Fixed-Point Number Representation
3.2.1 Format Choice
All FDTD arithmetic uses Q3.13 fixed-point format: 3 integer bits, 13 fractional bits, 1 sign bit = 17
bits total (use 18-bit to fit Xilinx DSP48E1 input width). This gives:
• Range: -4.0 to +3.9999 — field values normalised to fit this range
• Resolution: 2^-13 ≈ 0.000122 — sufficient for wave amplitudes
• Multiplication result: 36-bit intermediate, truncated back to 18-bit after multiply

3.2.2 The Update Coefficient
The key coefficient in all three update equations is c² × Δt/Δx (for the Ex and Ey updates) and
Δt/Δx (for the Bz update). With c_eff = 0.5 and Δt = 0.35, Δx = 1:
Cb_E = c_eff² × Δt / Δx = 0.25 × 0.35 = 0.0875
Cb_B = Δt / Δx = 0.35
These coefficients are stored as fixed-point constants in the Verilog — no division occurs during
the simulation loop.

3.2.3 Overflow Protection
Field values near sources can briefly exceed the Q3.13 range. Add saturation logic: if the result
of an addition exceeds the maximum representable value, clamp to the maximum. In Verilog this
is a simple comparison after each accumulation:
// Saturation example (18-bit Q3.13)
wire signed [17:0] result;
wire signed [17:0] saturated;
assign saturated = (result > 18'sh1FFFF) ? 18'sh1FFFF :
(result < -18'sh20000) ? -18'sh20000 : result;

3.3.2 The FDTD Engine Sequencer
The engine steps through the grid systematically. The sequencer is a state machine with the
following states:
1. IDLE — waiting for start signal from Python (new boundary conditions available)
2. UPDATE_BZ — iterate through all (i,j), compute and write new Bz. Takes NX×NY =
65,536 clock cycles.
3. INJECT_SOURCE — after Bz update, add source contributions at source positions.
Takes N_sources clock cycles.
4. APPLY_PML_BZ — multiply Bz at boundary cells by decay coefficient. Takes ~4×8×NX
clock cycles.
5. UPDATE_EX — iterate through all (i,j), compute and write new Ex. Takes NX×NY clock
cycles.
6. UPDATE_EY — iterate through all (i,j), compute and write new Ey. Takes NX×NY clock
cycles.
7. APPLY_PML_E — multiply Ex, Ey at boundary cells by decay coefficient.
8. STEP_DONE — increment time step counter, signal renderer to start, go to IDLE or
loop.
Total clock cycles per time step: approximately 4 × 65,536 + overhead ≈ 270,000 clock cycles. At
100MHz: 2.7ms per time step. At 60fps (16.7ms per frame): approximately 6 full FDTD time steps
per frame. This gives visibly smooth wave animation.

3.4 The Boundary Conditions — Physical Measurement
This is where the physical conducting paper measurement connects to the FDTD simulation.

3.4.1 What the Measurement Provides

The conducting paper with a hand probe gives you (x, y, V_measured) — the potential at any
point the user touches. The Python layer on the Pynq CPU receives this via the resistive
touchscreen's ADC outputs and the probe ADC reading.

3.4.2 How to Use Measurement as Boundary Condition
The measured potential defines the E field at the electrode positions. In the FDTD simulation,
these become Dirichlet boundary conditions — grid cells where the field is forced to a fixed value
rather than being updated by the FDTD equations.
For each electrode at position (xe, ye) with voltage Ve:
•
•
•

The Ex and Ey values at (xe, ye) are held fixed at values consistent with Ve
The FDTD update equation is NOT applied at these cells — they are skipped
The surrounding cells ARE updated normally — they respond to the electrode's fixed
field
In Verilog, this is implemented by a boundary condition mask — a 256×256 bit array stored in
block RAM where a '1' indicates a fixed cell and a '0' indicates a free cell updated by FDTD. The
update sequencer checks this mask before applying the update equation.

3.4.3 From Voltage to Field Value
The measured voltage V maps to Ex and Ey at the electrode position. Since E = -∇V, a positive
voltage region surrounded by lower voltage has E pointing outward. For the FDTD initialisation:
Ex at electrode ≈ -(V_right - V_left) / (2Δx)
Ey at electrode ≈ -(V_above - V_below) / (2Δy)
Where the surrounding values come from the Laplace solution (the conducting paper
measurement). This correctly initialises the E field consistent with the measured potential.

3.4.4 The Comparison Mode
In comparison mode, the FPGA simultaneously shows:
• Left panel: static Laplace solution from physical measurement (Jacobi solver or direct
measurement)
• Right panel: FDTD dynamic solution evolving from the same initial conditions
Initially they are identical. As the FDTD evolves, it shows how the field responds dynamically to
changes — wave propagation, radiation, ringing — that the static Laplace solution cannot show.
This directly motivates why Maxwell's equations are needed beyond Laplace.

3.5 Poynting Vector in FDTD
In FDTD, the Poynting vector is computed from the time-averaged fields. Since E and B are offset
by half a time step in the leapfrog scheme, you must interpolate to get them at the same instant
before taking the cross product.

3.5.1 Time Averaging
E_avg^n = (E^(n-½) + E^(n+½)) / 2

≈

For the TE mode with only Bz as the magnetic component:

|S| = √(Sx² + Sy²) / μ₀ = |E × B| / μ₀
This is computed per pixel by the Poynting calculator module and stored in a separate block RAM
for the renderer.

3.5.2 Time-Averaged Poynting Vector
For a sinusoidal steady state (after the simulation has run for many cycles), the time-averaged
Poynting vector is more physically meaningful than the instantaneous value:
<S> = (1/2) Re(E × B*) / μ₀
In FDTD this is approximated by accumulating the instantaneous Sx and Sy over one full source
period and dividing. The FPGA maintains running averages using a sliding window accumulator
— subtract the oldest value, add the newest, divide by window length. This gives a smooth, stable
Poynting vector display that shows the net energy flow direction rather than the oscillating
instantaneous values.

PART 4 — 3D VISUALISATION

4. 3D Visualisation of FDTD Fields
4.1 What Each Field Looks Like as a 3D Surface
4.1.1 Ex(x,y) and Ey(x,y) — Electric Field Components
Ex and Ey oscillate between positive and negative values as the wave propagates. As 3D surfaces
they look like rippling sheets — peaks and valleys sweeping across the grid as the wave moves.
Near a point source, the surface shows circular ripples propagating outward, diminishing in
amplitude with distance. For two sources, the surface shows an interference pattern — alternating
ridges and valleys where the waves constructively and destructively interfere.

4.1.2 Bz(x,y) — Magnetic Field
In TE mode, Bz is perpendicular to the simulation plane. Its 3D surface also shows the wave
propagation pattern but phase-shifted by 90° relative to E in the direction of propagation. Near
the source, Bz is strongest where Ex and Ey are weakest — the energy oscillates between E and
B storage as the wave propagates. Far from the source, E and B have equal amplitude — this is
the far-field condition where the wave is a plane wave.

4.1.3 |S|(x,y) — Poynting Vector Magnitude
The Poynting surface shows where power flows most intensely. Near the source it shows a
complex near-field pattern with energy oscillating back and forth. In the far field it shows smooth,
outward-flowing power. Between two interfering sources, the Poynting surface shows bright
fringes where constructive interference directs energy and dark fringes where destructive
interference blocks it. This is the most physically informative surface — it shows where the
electromagnetic energy actually goes.

4.1.4 The Time Dimension
Unlike the static Laplace surfaces, the FDTD surfaces are animated — they change every frame
as the wave evolves. The user sees the wave propagate outward from the source in real time.
Moving an electrode (source) shows a new wave front emanating from the new position. Changing
the drive frequency visibly changes the wavelength on screen. This animation is the most powerful
demonstration of the FPGA's advantage over the static Laplace approach.

4.2 Rendering the Animated 3D Surface
4.2.1 The Challenge Versus Static Rendering
For the static Laplace case, the field changes slowly (only when electrodes move). For FDTD, the
field changes every frame — the entire surface reshapes 60 times per second. The renderer must
read the complete field BRAM, build the mesh, project all vertices, and rasterise all triangles within
16.7ms. This is tight but achievable for a 256×256 grid with careful pipelining.

4.2.2 Reduced Resolution Rendering
Rather than rendering all 256×256 = 65,536 points as a mesh (which would create 130,000
triangles — too many for real-time rendering), subsample the field to a 64×64 rendering grid.
Every 4th point in x and y is used, giving 4096 points and 8000 triangles. Intermediate values are
bilinearly interpolated for colour mapping, giving smooth colour even on the coarser render mesh.

4.2.3 False Colour for Oscillating Fields
Unlike the static Laplace surface where values are always positive, FDTD fields oscillate between
positive and negative. The colour map must handle this:
• Positive Ex: warm colours (yellow → red) — field pointing in +x direction
• Negative Ex: cool colours (cyan → blue) — field pointing in -x direction
• Zero: black or grey — field node
• For |S|: black → purple → orange → white — power always positive
This bipolar colour map is used in all professional EM simulation software. It immediately shows
wave crests (red) and troughs (blue) propagating across the grid.

4.2.4 Phase Animation
An alternative to height mapping for oscillating fields is to show the field purely through colour on
a flat grid — like a heat map that ripples. This is simpler to render (no 3D projection needed) and
can show the full 256×256 resolution without subsampling. Use this as a fallback mode if the full
3D rendering pipeline proves too slow.
Show both: a small 3D perspective view in one corner showing the surface shape, and a large
flat colour map showing full-resolution field animation. The combination gives both structural
information (3D view) and detailed wave pattern (2D map).

5. Physical Measurement Integration
5.1 Conducting Paper Setup
Teledeltos paper (graphite-coated conducting paper) is the resistive medium. It is uniform, stable,
and requires no water. Two electrodes clipped to opposite edges (or any positions on the paper)
create the boundary conditions. The user probes the surface with a thin metal tip connected to
the ADC.

position. When the probe tip presses through the panel onto the paper, the touch panel reports
(x_norm, y_norm) as two voltages on its Y+ and X+ pins, and the probe ADC reads the voltage V
at that point.

5.3 How the Measurement Feeds FDTD
The Python control loop runs at 50Hz and:
9. Reads the probe position (x, y) and voltage V from the three XADC channels.
10. Maps (x, y) from normalised 0–1 range to grid indices (0–255, 0–255).
11. Writes V to a boundary condition register: position (ix, iy) → voltage V.
12. Writes electrode A and B positions and voltages to boundary condition registers.
13. FPGA reads these registers and holds the corresponding grid cells at fixed values during
FDTD update.

The result: wherever the user touches the paper, a new boundary condition is injected into the
FDTD simulation at that grid point. Moving the probe across the paper in real time draws boundary
conditions into the simulation — like drawing with an electric potential.

5.5 The Interactive Wave Tank
Mode 4 above is the most impressive demo. The FDTD simulation runs continuously with a source

6.3 Simulation-First Development Strategy
Every FDTD Verilog module must be verified in software simulation before loading to hardware.
FPGA build times are 15–20 minutes — debugging on hardware is impractical. Use this workflow:
14. Write the Verilog module.
15. Write a testbench that exercises the module with known inputs.
16. Simulate with Icarus Verilog — verify outputs match hand-calculated values.
17. Run simulation for 1000+ time steps — verify wave propagates, doesn't blow up.
18. Check that energy is conserved (field amplitude doesn't grow without a source).
19. Only then generate the bitstream and load to hardware.
For the FDTD engine specifically: simulate a 1D version first (just a row of cells, waves
propagating left and right). This is much easier to debug than the full 2D case and verifies the
core arithmetic is correct.

7. Demo Scenarios — What the Examiner Sees
7.1 Scenario 1 — Static Field Validation
Start with FDTD in static mode (no AC drive). Place two electrodes on the conducting paper. The
FDTD simulation converges to the Laplace solution. The 3D surface on screen shows the classic
dipole potential landscape — one peak, one valley, smooth saddle between. Switch to
comparison mode: left panel shows the Laplace solver output, right panel shows the FDTD static
solution. They match. This validates the FDTD implementation and demonstrates that Maxwell's
equations reduce to Laplace in the static limit.

7.2 Scenario 2 — Wave Propagation
Switch to dynamic mode. The electrode is now driven at frequency f₀. Immediately, circular
wavefronts propagate outward from the source position across all three 3D surfaces. The Ex
surface shows the oscillating electric field rippling outward. The Bz surface shows the magnetic
field, 90° out of phase. The Poynting surface shows smooth outward energy flow — the radiation
pattern of a point source. The examiner can rotate all three surfaces simultaneously to inspect
the wave from any angle.

7.3 Scenario 3 — Two-Source Interference
A second source is activated (second electrode, driven at same frequency). Immediately the
interference pattern appears — the classic alternating fringes of constructive and destructive
interference. The Poynting surface is particularly striking: bright fringes where energy flows
strongly outward between the interference maxima, and dark nodes where destructive
interference prevents energy flow. This is the EM equivalent of Young's double-slit experiment,
computed in real time on the FPGA.

7.4 Scenario 4 — Interactive Wave Tank
The user picks up the conducting paper probe and touches it to the surface. A boundary condition
(a reflecting obstacle) is injected at that point. The wave pattern immediately diffracts around the
probe tip — visible as a shadow region behind the tip and curved wavefronts around its sides.
Moving the probe slowly shows the diffraction pattern tracking the obstacle in real time. This is
the most interactive and visually dramatic part of the demo.

7.5 Scenario 5 — Poynting Vector Deep Dive
With two sources creating an interference pattern, switch to Poynting-only full-screen mode. The
time-averaged Poynting vector magnitude fills the screen as a 3D surface. Peaks of power flow
appear between the interference maxima — channels where electromagnetic energy is directed
by the interference pattern. The user rotates the surface to see the power flow landscape from all
angles. This directly demonstrates the project's title claim: we can visualise where
electromagnetic energy goes, not just where the fields are strong.

The Central Message of the Demo
Laplace's equation tells you where charges sit in equilibrium. Maxwell's equations tell you how
electromagnetic energy moves through space and time. The FPGA solves Maxwell's equations
— 65,536 coupled differential equations simultaneously — fast enough to show the solution
animating in real time at 60 frames per second. A CPU cannot do this. That is why the FPGA is
here.
